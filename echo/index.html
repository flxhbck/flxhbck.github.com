Echo

Networks are shaped, shifted, defined by movements. If we consider them as planes, they exist within certain boundaries, frontiers that separate one from one another. They are contained within edges that sometime get dull or sharpen, that sometime extend or break. A malleable matrix of push-pull dynamics, a self defined entity reviving itself continuously. Scar tissues are networks.

Terrains as body. Ridges as networks.

Abstract

Echo physically visualizes invisible tectonics plates : the unstable virtual ground. It's silent but far from inert. Most see it as a whole medium, as an homogeneously stable mixture of media when in fact, it react like nitroglycerin. Billion times a second. How can we render those crossings, those collisions, that noise? The same as if they would be actual earthquakes; using cross-references as signs that tectonics plates are shearing, digital movements are translated visually using a seismograph.

Idea

Imagine major sites as continents, as moving geologic systems. Their size dictating their area, their volume. When one reference to another one, there is displacement: moving references that "push" traffic to the edges where they can cross between different soil. That's what is going to be physically mapped by our machine. Echo concentrates its effort on Twitter, as it is one of the most active system right now when one think about cross referencing. Every time a Twitter post references another network, the seismograph graphs it.

The viewer is able to contemplate directly how precarious digital soil really is using our instrument. Himself an inhabitant, he is invited to witness the shifting nature of his online foundations, of his networked references. Questioning those rapids displacements will let him assess the ephemeral nature of online ground and hopefully, muse on how he spawn those change himself.
